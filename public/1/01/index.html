Link: NA Description: Estimated spending shares by age group Comment: Estimated share of spending by age, based on past service expenditure - Data takem from Revenue Outturn forms 2014⁄15 to 2016⁄17 (i.e. 3 years). Net current expenditure. - Expenditure was mapped to the following age bands: - Ages 0-17: Children’s services - Ages 18-64: Younger adult social care - Ages 65+: Older adult social care - All ages: Housing (GFRA only), Cultural & Related, Environmental & Regulatory, Planning & Development, Central Services, ‘Other’ expenditure. - Education, Highways & Transport, Public Health, Police and Fire were excluded from the analysis - Some expenditure on adult social is not clearly attributable to an age band. This was therefore apportioned pro rata to each authority’s known expenditure on the 18-64 and 65+ age bands. - For shire districts, it is assumed that all expenditure is attributable to ‘all ages’. A dot-density map of Dallas

 May 5, 2018
As a new arrival in Dallas, it’s taken me a while to get to grips with its layout. Its sheer size and sprawl make it difficult to identify where Dallas ends and neighboring cities begin. Expanses of new developments and construction appear to have blurred the lines between what were once distinct towns and cities. With this in mind, this post has two aims: first, to better understand the geography of Dallas, and second, to do so while exploring some of the GIS tools available in R, the open-source programming language.

Funding cuts and deprivation in England

 Apr 4, 2018
The most deprived parts of England have borne the brunt of public spending cuts since 2010. As spending now begins to increase, this could bring relief to some of the hardest-hit local councils. However, this analysis shows that the gains - unlike the cuts - will be shared fairly evenly among both affluent and deprived parts of England. In 2010 the UK government embarked on a series of major spending cuts to reduce its budget deficit, in response to the financial crisis.
